Understanding stroke, stage by stage

A short reference for patients and families on what a stroke is, who's at risk, and what care typically looks like.

STAGE01

Recognize — what a stroke actually is

A stroke cuts off blood flow to part of the brain. Without oxygen, brain cells begin dying within minutes, which can cause lasting damage or disability. About 80% of strokes are ischemic, caused by a clot blocking a vessel. The rest are hemorrhagic, caused by a vessel that ruptures and bleeds into the brain.

A transient ischemic attack (TIA), sometimes called a "mini-stroke," produces the same warning signs but resolves on its own. It still signals a much higher risk of a full stroke soon after.

STAGE02

Risk — who's most exposed

High blood pressure is the leading risk factor. Diabetes, atrial fibrillation and other heart disease, smoking, a personal or family history of stroke or TIA, and increasing age all raise risk further.

  • Heavy alcohol or drug use, and physical inactivity
  • High cholesterol and an unhealthy diet
  • Obesity
STAGE03

Symptoms — beyond FAST

Stroke symptoms tend to appear suddenly. Beyond facial drooping, arm weakness, and slurred speech, watch for:

  • Sudden numbness or weakness, especially on one side of the body
  • Sudden confusion or trouble understanding speech
  • Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes
  • Sudden dizziness, loss of balance, or difficulty walking
  • A sudden, severe headache with no known cause
Any of these → call emergency services immediately
STAGE04

Diagnosis — confirming what happened

Clinicians review symptoms and history, run a focused neurological exam, then confirm with imaging — typically a CT or MRI scan, alongside blood tests or an angiogram. This is the step NeuroPulse assists: flagging stroke-positive scans so they reach a clinician's review faster.

STAGE05

Treatment — what happens next

For ischemic stroke, clot-dissolving medication or, in severe cases, mechanical clot removal (thrombectomy) can restore blood flow. For hemorrhagic stroke, treatment may involve surgery to repair the vessel, plus medication to control blood pressure or prevent seizures. Rehabilitation — physical, occupational, and speech therapy — supports recovery afterward.

STAGE06

Prevention — reducing the odds

Controlling blood pressure, managing diabetes, quitting smoking, limiting alcohol, staying active, and maintaining a healthy weight all meaningfully lower stroke risk. Where prescribed, medications like aspirin or statins help too — paired with regular checkups to track risk factors over time.
